Frequently asked questions

How far is it from Earth to Mars right now?

It depends entirely on where each planet currently sits in its orbit. At closest approach the two planets are around 56 million kilometres apart; on opposite sides of the Sun, that stretches past 400 million kilometres. The calculator works out today's figure live. See also: why the distance changes so much.

How long does light take to travel from the Sun to each planet?

About 8 minutes 20 seconds to Earth, roughly 4.3 minutes to Mercury, around 12.5 minutes to Mars, and over 4 hours to Neptune — though the exact figure shifts slightly as each planet moves through its orbit. More detail: how the Sun's light-minutes are worked out.

What planet is closest to Earth right now?

It changes constantly. Venus, Mars, and Mercury each take turns being the nearest planet, depending on where all three bodies happen to be in their orbits on a given day. See which planet is actually closest to Earth on average.

Why does the site show a distance that looks different from what I read elsewhere?

Most published "distance to Mars" figures you see quoted are the average or closest-approach distance, not today's actual distance. This site calculates the current position of each body and reports the real, live distance, which is almost never the same as the average.

How accurate are these numbers?

Planet positions come from the VSOP87 model, accurate to within a few percent of true range. That's precise enough to understand scale and trends, but not precise enough for navigation or scientific publication — see the terms and conditions for the full note.
